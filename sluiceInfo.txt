## One-liner

Balancor is a decentralized market for routing policies: a Bittensor-native coordination layer where miners compete to decide the best place to send an AI request based on user goals such as cost, latency, quality, and privacy.

## Executive summary

AI demand is fragmenting across model APIs, specialized subnets, data pipelines, and private compute providers. The hard problem is no longer only **how to compute**; it is increasingly **where each request should go**.

Balancor turns this routing decision into a competitive market. Users or applications submit a request plus constraints. Miners generate routing decisions. Validators benchmark those decisions against live execution and reference alternatives, then reward the routes that best satisfy the objective function.

This makes Balancor a meta-infrastructure subnet: it does not try to outcompete every model provider. Instead, it helps the network discover, price, and route to the best provider for each job.

## The problem

### AI infrastructure is becoming fragmented

- The best provider changes by task, price, model quality, latency, data locality, and privacy requirements.
- Developers still rely on static routing, manual provider selection, or centralized black-box routers.
- In Bittensor specifically, value is being created across many specialized subnets, but there is no neutral routing layer that coordinates demand across them efficiently.

### What breaks today

- Users overpay because they choose a familiar provider instead of the optimal one.
- Latency-sensitive applications cannot dynamically trade off cost vs speed.
- Privacy-sensitive workloads need more nuanced routing than a single API endpoint can provide.
- Bittensor subnets become harder to navigate as the ecosystem expands.

## Our thesis

The winning coordination layer in decentralized AI will not be the provider that computes everything itself. It will be the marketplace that decides where computation should go.

Balancor is building that marketplace.

## Product

### What Balancor does

Balancor accepts an inference request together with constraints such as:

- budget ceiling
- minimum quality target
- preferred latency band
- privacy sensitivity
- provider exclusions or routing preferences

Miners compete to output the best routing policy for that request.

Validators score those policies by comparing the chosen route against actual execution results and reference benchmarks across candidate providers.

### Core product promise

For every request, Balancor aims to answer:

**What is the cheapest route that still satisfies the user’s quality, speed, and privacy requirements?**

## Why this matters for Bittensor

- It increases utilization across the broader subnet ecosystem.
- It creates a demand-discovery layer above fragmented model and compute supply.
- It turns routing intelligence into a measurable, rewardable on-chain behavior.
- It strengthens Bittensor’s long-term value by making the network easier for applications to consume.

Balancor is not just another application subnet. It is infrastructure that can make many other subnets more useful.

## How the subnet works

### Input layer

A user or application sends:

- prompt or task metadata
- budget constraint
- latency target
- privacy preference
- optional model or provider preferences

### Routing layer

Miners act as decision engines. Each miner predicts the best route for the task by evaluating expected:

- output quality
- total cost
- response latency
- route reliability
- privacy fit

### Execution and scoring layer

Validators:

- execute the selected route
- compare outcomes against benchmark routes
- estimate regret versus the best available alternative
- score miners based on objective performance

### Example scoring framework

A simplified scoring function could be:

Score = 0.50 × quality + 0.20 × reliability + 0.15 × privacy fit - 0.10 × cost deviation - 0.05 × latency deviation

The exact weights can evolve over time by workload type.

## What makes this defensible

### 1. Evaluation moat

The hardest part is not proposing a route. It is **proving that the route was optimal enough** under real-world conditions. A strong benchmark and scoring layer becomes a durable moat.

### 2. Data flywheel

Each request improves the network’s understanding of:

- which providers perform best on which task types
- how prices and latency shift over time
- what tradeoffs users actually care about

Over time, Balancor accumulates a valuable routing-performance dataset that improves decision quality.

### 3. Network effects

More providers increase route diversity.

More miners increase strategy diversity.

More validators improve scoring credibility.

More users create better signal.

This makes the market harder to replicate as adoption grows.

## Why now

- The number of models, inference APIs, and decentralized compute options is increasing rapidly.
- Enterprises and applications are under pressure to reduce inference spend without sacrificing product quality.
- Bittensor is becoming increasingly specialized, which raises the value of a coordination layer.
- Privacy-aware and latency-aware routing are becoming product requirements, not nice-to-haves.

## Business model

### 1. Savings-share software revenue

Balancor can sell a routing layer to applications and keep a percentage of verified savings.

Example:

- customer spends $10,000 per month on inference
- Balancor reduces effective spend by $4,000 while meeting product constraints
- Balancor captures 10–15% of savings as software revenue

This aligns pricing with customer value rather than raw API volume.

### 2. Premium routing tiers

Higher-margin features can include:

- privacy-focused routing
- low-latency routing
- dedicated enterprise policy controls
- reliability guarantees and fallback orchestration

### 3. Treasury and owner economics

A launched subnet can also accrue protocol-native economics through owner emissions and treasury strategy. This should be treated as upside and strategic alignment, not the sole business case.

## Go-to-market

### Initial wedge

Start with AI-native teams and agents that already use multiple providers and feel routing pain daily.

### Beachhead users

- agent builders
- inference-heavy startups
- teams using model ensembles or fallbacks
- developers building on Bittensor who need provider abstraction

### Distribution strategy

- launch a simple API and dashboard for routing recommendations
- publish benchmark reports showing measurable savings and latency improvements
- integrate with agent frameworks and developer tooling
- use the subnet itself as proof of routing performance

## Product roadmap

### Phase 1: MVP

- Route based on task metadata and structured preferences
- Validators run benchmark execution across a small provider set
- Focus on cost, latency, and basic quality scoring
- No raw-prompt sharing required for the first version where avoidable

### Phase 2: Production-grade router

- Expand provider coverage across APIs and selected Bittensor services
- Introduce dynamic policy controls and route fallback logic
- Build developer-facing API, dashboard, and analytics
- Add enterprise billing and savings reporting

### Phase 3: Privacy and trust layer

- Encrypted routing where possible
- Trusted execution or privacy-preserving routing paths for sensitive workloads
- Stronger auditability around why a route was chosen

### Phase 4: Advanced market design

- Workload-specific scoring models
- Multi-hop routing across data, model, and post-processing layers
- Open policy marketplace where routing strategies themselves become composable assets

## Capital deployment and recovery logic

### Use of funds

Capital raised would be used to:

- build the validator benchmark infrastructure
- launch and harden the subnet
- bootstrap first-party routing quality during the cold-start phase
- ship the developer API and observability tooling
- fund early customer acquisition and ecosystem integrations

### Recovery philosophy

The capital recovery path should come from **multiple aligned sources**, not one speculative assumption:

- owner emissions
- first-party operational participation during bootstrap
- validator and treasury strategy where appropriate
- eventual software revenue from routing customers

### Important framing

For investor-facing discussions, emissions should be framed as one component of the economic stack, not the entire thesis. The stronger primary story is that Balancor can become a revenue-generating coordination product with protocol-native upside.

## Risks and mitigations

### Risk: route quality is hard to measure

Mitigation: invest heavily in benchmark design, offline evaluation, and live regret measurement.

### Risk: providers change pricing and performance quickly

Mitigation: treat routing as a continuously updated market problem, not a static policy engine.

### Risk: cold-start network quality

Mitigation: bootstrap with first-party routing and validator infrastructure until external miner competition becomes meaningful.

### Risk: enterprises may hesitate on decentralization

Mitigation: offer hybrid paths, auditable policies, and enterprise-friendly controls before leading with ideology.
